Part I · Foundations · §2

From similarity to attention.

A list of dot-product scores is not yet attention. We need to turn raw numbers into weights that sum to one — a probability distribution over what to look at. Softmax does this, with two surprisingly subtle properties.

The definition

Softmax with temperature
$$ \mathrm{softmax}(x)_i \;=\; \frac{e^{x_i / T}}{\sum_{j} e^{x_j / T}} $$

Properties worth memorizing:

  • Output is a probability distribution. All entries are in $(0,1)$ and sum to $1$.
  • Translation-invariant. $\mathrm{softmax}(x + c\mathbf{1}) = \mathrm{softmax}(x)$ — only differences matter.
  • Temperature controls sharpness. $T \to 0$ approaches one-hot argmax; $T \to \infty$ approaches uniform.

Why exponentials?

Why not just $x_i / \sum_j x_j$? Two reasons. First, scores can be negative — straight normalization breaks. Second, exponentials make the function smooth and differentiable, with a clean gradient: $\partial \mathrm{softmax}_i / \partial x_j = p_i(\delta_{ij} - p_j)$. That gradient is the only reason the network can learn at all.

Experiment 3 — The gradient

Why exponentials make learning possible.

The Jacobian $\partial p_i / \partial x_j = p_i(\delta_{ij} - p_j)$. The diagonal is $p_i(1-p_i)$ — maximized at $p_i = 0.5$, vanishing when $p_i$ is $0$ or $1$. This is why one-hot outputs kill training.

Fig 3 — push the peak score up. Probability concentrates, gradient evaporates. This is the same mechanism that motivates the $\sqrt{d_k}$ divisor in §3.2.
To remember — Softmax converts scores into a distribution, smoothly. Two facts to keep close: it ignores additive shifts, and its gradient dies as the output approaches one-hot.
