Part III · §6

The Transformer block, wired up.

We have every ingredient. Now we assemble. A Transformer block is one attention sub-layer, one feed-forward sub-layer, two residuals, two LayerNorms. Stack $N$ of them and you have GPT.

The block, in formulas

Pre-norm Transformer block
$$ x' = x + \mathrm{MultiHead}(\mathrm{LN}(x)) $$ $$ y = x' + \mathrm{FFN}(\mathrm{LN}(x')) $$

Two residual streams. Two LayerNorms. The original "Attention Is All You Need" paper uses post-norm ($\mathrm{LN}(x + \text{sublayer}(x))$); modern LLMs almost universally switched to pre-norm — it trains more stably at depth.

Experiment 3 — Pre-norm vs. post-norm

One bracket moved, training behavior changes.

Two equations. They look almost identical:

Original (post-norm)
$$ x' = \mathrm{LN}(x + \mathrm{Sublayer}(x)) $$
Modern (pre-norm)
$$ x' = x + \mathrm{Sublayer}(\mathrm{LN}(x)) $$

In post-norm the residual flows through LayerNorm. In pre-norm there's an unbroken identity path from input to output of every layer — the residual stream — so gradients flow cleanly even through a stack of 100+ blocks. Every modern LLM (GPT, LLaMA, Mistral) is pre-norm.

To remember — attention is the routing mechanism, FFN is the memory. Residuals carry information across layers; LayerNorm keeps activations from blowing up. Stack the block 96 times and you have GPT-3.
