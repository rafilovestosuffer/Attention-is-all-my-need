Part II · Scaled dot-product attention · §3.2.1

Why we scale by √dk.

Vaswani et al. include one tiny divisor that makes the difference between a Transformer that learns and one that doesn't. Below: the proof, three live experiments, and the gradient consequence in numbers you can drag.

The claim

Suppose query q and key k are dk-dimensional vectors with i.i.d. components, each with mean 0 and variance 1. Then:

Lemma — variance of the dot product
$$ \mathbb{E}[\,q \cdot k\,] = 0, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(q \cdot k) = d_k $$

Proof in one line. The dot product is $q \cdot k = \sum_{i=1}^{d_k} q_i k_i$. Each $q_i k_i$ has mean $0$ and variance $\mathbb{E}[q_i^2]\mathbb{E}[k_i^2] = 1$ (since $q_i \perp k_i$, both unit-variance). Independence makes variances add: $\mathrm{Var}(q \cdot k) = \sum_i 1 = d_k$. So the standard deviation grows like $\sqrt{d_k}$.

The fix: divide by $\sqrt{d_k}$. The variance returns to $1$, regardless of dimension. Softmax now sees inputs at a constant scale as the model gets wider.

Experiment 1 — Empirical variance

Sample 5,000 dot products. Watch the variance.

We'll draw $q, k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_{d_k})$, compute $q \cdot k$ five thousand times, and plot the empirical distribution. Theory predicts $\mathrm{Var} = d_k$ unscaled, $\mathrm{Var} = 1$ scaled.

Experiment 2 — Softmax collapse

What does softmax do to a high-variance input?

Experiment 3 — The gradient that vanishes

This is why training would fail without it.

Softmax's gradient is $\partial L/\partial x_i = p_i (\mathbb{1}_{i=t} - p_t)$ where $p$ is the output and $t$ the target. If $p$ is one-hot, every component of this vector is essentially zero — no signal flows to $W_Q$, $W_K$, $W_V$. The unscaled side trains to a halt.

The takeaway

One line. Three consequences.

The $\sqrt{d_k}$ divisor is doing all of these at once:

  1. Holds variance constant. $\mathrm{Var}(q \cdot k / \sqrt{d_k}) = 1$ regardless of model width.
  2. Prevents softmax saturation. Inputs stay in the regime where multiple values get meaningful probability mass.
  3. Enables gradient flow. $\partial \mathrm{softmax}$ is nontrivial only when the output is not one-hot.

It's a one-character change with a load-bearing role. Vaswani et al. didn't invent the dot product, didn't invent softmax, didn't invent attention. But they noticed this — and that's why the paper works.

To remember — $\sqrt{d_k}$ keeps softmax's effective temperature constant as the model gets wider. Without it, attention sharpens automatically with depth and the gradient dies.
