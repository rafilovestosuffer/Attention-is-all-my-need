Part II · §4.4

Eight heads, eight readings.

A single attention head can express only one relationship at a time. Multi-head attention runs $h$ of them in parallel, each with its own learned $W_Q^{(i)}, W_K^{(i)}, W_V^{(i)}$. The model gets to attend to many things at once.

The formula

Multi-head attention
$$ \mathrm{MultiHead}(Q,K,V) \;=\; \mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1, \ldots, \mathrm{head}_h)\,W^O $$ $$ \mathrm{head}_i \;=\; \mathrm{Attention}(QW_Q^{(i)},\, KW_K^{(i)},\, VW_V^{(i)}) $$

Three things to note. First, each head operates in a lower-dimensional subspace ($d_k = d_{\text{model}}/h$). Second, total compute is roughly the same as a single head with the full $d_{\text{model}}$. Third, $W^O$ mixes the heads back into one $d_{\text{model}}$-dim output.

Why parallel heads instead of one big one? Subspace specialization. Each head's projection picks out a different subspace of the input — one might track syntactic dependency, another co-reference, another semantic role. With $h=1$, the single softmax has to compromise across all of these.

Experiment 2 — Compute cost is constant in $h$

Splitting into heads is free.

Every head works in $d_k = d_{\text{model}} / h$ dimensions. Total FLOPs:

$$ h \cdot (n^2 d_k) \;=\; h \cdot n^2 \cdot \frac{d_{\text{model}}}{h} \;=\; n^2 \cdot d_{\text{model}} $$

The $h$ cancels. We get more expressive power for the same FLOP budget — which is why the original paper just picks $h = 8$ and moves on.

To remember — Multi-head is parallel attention in disjoint subspaces, free at the FLOP level, paid for in expressivity. Eight heads, eight stories about the same sentence.
