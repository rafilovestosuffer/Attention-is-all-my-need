Part I · Foundations · §1

A vector, a dot product.

Before "attention" is anything, it is a way of asking how similar two vectors are. The dot product is the smallest piece of the Transformer — and the only operation that matters for the next four chapters.

The definition

For two vectors $a, b \in \mathbb{R}^d$, the dot product is the sum of element-wise products:

Definition
$$ a \cdot b \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{d} a_i\, b_i \;=\; \|a\| \, \|b\| \, \cos\theta $$

Two equivalent forms — algebraic (a sum) and geometric (a product of lengths and an angle's cosine). The equivalence is what makes the dot product useful: it lets us read geometry off a stream of numbers.

Three cases worth holding in your head:

  • $a \cdot b > 0$ — the vectors point in roughly the same direction (acute angle).
  • $a \cdot b = 0$ — they are orthogonal; one carries no information about the other.
  • $a \cdot b < 0$ — they point in opposite directions (obtuse).

Experiment 2 — Random pairs in $\mathbb{R}^d$

In high dimensions, random vectors are nearly orthogonal.

This is the fact that makes attention possible. Sample pairs $a, b$ with $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ entries; compute $\cos\theta = (a \cdot b) / (\|a\| \|b\|)$. As $d$ grows, the distribution of $\cos\theta$ concentrates around $0$ — meaning random vectors carry almost no signal about each other. Learned vectors don't have to compete with random noise.

Experiment 3 — Why this is computationally beautiful

One operation, every framework, every chip.

An $n$-token sequence with $d$-dim embeddings produces an $n \times n$ score matrix from a single matrix multiply: $S = QK^\top$. That's $n^2 d$ multiply-adds — a workload modern hardware was literally designed for.

n = 512, d = 64≈ 17M
n = 2048, d = 64≈ 268M
n = 8192, d = 64≈ 4.3B

The $n^2$ scaling — quadratic in sequence length — is exactly what people mean by "the attention bottleneck." Whole research subfields exist to dodge it (FlashAttention, linear attention, sparse attention). For now, accept it: attention is fast enough that we can afford the cost in exchange for the modeling power.

To remember — The dot product is similarity. In high dimensions it's almost always small for random pairs, so any large value is meaningful signal.
